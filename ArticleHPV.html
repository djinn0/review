Sex Toys and HPV: safety tips and risks?

What is HPV?

Human Papillomavirus or HPV for short are a family of several hundred related viruses. Most HPV strains are either asymptomatic or cause mild symptoms and are naturally cleared by the body within 1 to 2 years. Some strains of HPV cause more serious symptoms like genital warts and cancer. HPV is the most common sexually transmitted infection and approximately 80% of the population will contract it sometime during their lives. HPV is usually transmitted during vaginal, oral or anal sex or through close skin-to-skin contact during sex. Some HPV subtypes can be transmitted on surfaces, but this occurs far less frequently then sexual transmission. Symptoms of HPV such as warts can be treated, but there is currently no treatment for the virus itself. There is a vaccine that protects against the most dangerous HPV subtypes.

How does HPV cause cancer?

HPV infects the cells in the lowest layer of skin. As these cells replicate to produce new skin cells the virus is copied as well. The virus modifies its host cells so that it can continue to replicate within them. These modifications cause the infected cells to continue grow and separate which can create lumps of skin cells called warts. These modifications can also change the cells DNA inducing cancer. Two subtypes of HPV, strains 16 and 18, are particularly adept at inducing cancer and cause over 75% of HPV derived cancers.

Potential for HPV transmission on sex toys.

HPV is a very durable virus. It is a nonenveloped virus which means that it is not deactivated by drying out and can remain infectious on the surface. Researchers found traces of HPV on medical equipment after it had been cleaned with hospital grade disinfectant. In another study HPV was detected on a vibrator 24 hours after use, which shows that HPV could potentally be transmitted on sex toys.

How to minimize risk of HPV infection

Unfortunately, I do not have the resources to demonstrate that sex toys can transmit HPV or test methods for disinfecting contaminated toys so we will have to rely on previously published research. Surface transmission of STI's is an understudied field of research bit there are a couple of relevant studies. I think that studies done on disinfecting HPV contaminated medical supplies are applicable to sex toys. I also feel that practices recomended for reducing HPV transmission during partnered sex are applicable to sex toy use. I have provided a few ideas to minimize risk of HPV transmission based on current studies and best practice.

  • Do not share toys
    • This is the simplest method of avoiding HPV transmission. A toy can not transmit HPV to another person if it is not shared.
  • Get Vaccinated
    • A safe and effective vaccine has been developed against the most dangerous subtypes of HPV. The CDC recomends vaccination for everyone age 12-26. The CDC also recommends adults older than 26 who have a higher risk of HPV infection get vaccinated. Vaccination protects against high risk strains that could be contracted during sex or through sharing toys.
  • Use a condom or other barrier
    • HPV is transmited through skin-to-skin or surface-to-skin contact. A Condom can act as a barrier preventing virus transference from a contaminated surface. Condoms are more effective at preventing HPV infection on sex toys than in partnered sex because they can completely cover the toy providing an effective barrier.
  • Thoroughly clean sex toys
    • Soap and water do not deactivate HPV but they can wash it off of a surface. Sex toys should be washed after use to remove microbes and keep them clean and sanitary. Thoroughly scrubbing a sex toy with soap and water can remove HPV from the surface reducing the risk of transmission.
  • UV Sterilization
    • UV sterilization has been shown to be effective at deactivating HPV and decontaminating surfaces. After discovering that standard cleaning procedures were ineffective at deactivating HPV most Obstetrician and Gynecologist offices switched to using UV to sterilize their tools. If UV is effective at sterilizing medical tools it should also be fairly effective at sterilizing sex toys. Bvibe sells a UV sterilizer designed specifically for sex toys.

Conclusion

The Risk of contracting HPV through Sex toy use is likely pretty low, but I think it is valuable to be informed about any potential risks. Research has shown that medical tools that are inserted vaginally are a potential vector for transmitting HPV between individuals. Studies have also shown that traces of HPV are detectable on Sex toys after use by HPV positive individuals so it follows that sex toys could act as a vector for HPV transmission. Many of the commonly employed methods for cleaning sex toys, like boiling or using alcohol based cleaners are ineffective at deactivating HPV. I think that decontamination methods used in the medical field, and principles for avoiding HPV transmission during partnered sex are applicable to sex toys, and can greatly reduce transmission risks.
